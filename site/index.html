the approach

Why another look at the Indus script?

For a century people have claimed to read this script. Most claims share one flaw: they start by assuming a language, then force the signs to fit it. Assume hard enough and the same signs can be made to read as Sanskrit, Tamil, or Old English. That is not decipherment. It is confirmation bias with extra steps.

the old approach

Assume a language

Pick a language first, then bend the signs until they spell something in it. Allow enough freedom and almost any short text can be made to "read."

our approach

Assume nothing

We asked only what the arrangement of signs can prove on its own, and where that evidence runs out. No sound is assigned to any sign, anywhere on this site.

what that buys you

A smaller, honest answer

Where earlier work asked "what could this say?", we asked "what can we prove, and what can we never prove from these seals alone?" The second question has a smaller, more honest answer. This site is that answer.

the method

Show me the math, in plain words.

We mention mathematics all over this site. Here is the whole idea, with no equations, in five steps.

1

Take thousands of real inscriptions.

Around 2,500 short Indus texts, each a row of signs read right to left.

2

Notice the order.

In real texts, certain signs almost always sit at the end, others at the start. There is a fixed slot order, like the way a title follows a name.

3

Now shuffle each text at random.

Keep the same signs, scramble their positions. If the order were meaningless, shuffling should change nothing.

4

Watch the order collapse.

75% of real texts obey the slot rule. Only 23% of shuffled ones do. That gap is the proof. A pattern that survives in real texts but dies when shuffled is a real rule, not a coincidence.

5

Do this for every claim.

Survive the shuffle, or it does not count. Every result here is tested this way. See The Wall for what survives, and The Ledger to test a reading yourself.

04 · the rebus trap

How a false decipherment is born.

In plain terms: watch a tempting reading take shape, then watch us break it on purpose.

DECODED

The same trap, four ways.

The fish is one case. Here is the pattern behind most failed readings, on every side of the debate.

The assumption trap

Assume a language first and you will always find it. Decide the script is Language X, and ambiguous signs can be bent until they spell words in X. The catch: the same signs bend just as easily to Language Y.

the check: does it assume the answer it claims to find?

The look-alike trap

Merge signs that look similar and the alphabet shrinks until your language fits. But the merges were chosen to make the reading work, which is circular reasoning in a lab coat.

the check: were the sign merges fixed before or after the reading?

The cherry-pick trap

Show the five seals that read beautifully, hide the five thousand that turn to nonsense. A reading that works on a flattering sample is not a decipherment.

the check: does it handle the whole corpus, or just the highlights?

The no-control trap

Any clever person can find some pattern. The only real test is whether it survives against shuffled, random controls. Skip the control and you will always succeed, at nothing.

the check: did it beat a random baseline?

We are not immune to these. That is why every claim here runs against controls, and why our own favorite reading, fish as star, is marked wounded. The discipline is the point, not the conclusion.

07 · the claims ledger

Run your theory through the gauntlet.

The Indus script has no Rosetta Stone, so anyone can claim anything. We cannot verify meanings, but we can test whether a proposed reading survives structural falsification. Here is the open bar. Clear it.

(a) beat the shuffle

A real reading's texts must be far less shuffle-invariant than random order, the slot grammar has to constrain them.

(b) survive the null model

Any rebus must beat a frequency-matched permutation null, not merely find one homophone that fits.

(c) no subjective merging

It cannot depend on hand-merging signs until the statistics happen to work.

The code is open. One command reproduces every verdict below: python3 run.py  ·  open repository

How to contribute

Submit a claim as a GitHub pull request against the open benchmark. Code review is the moderation layer, the way the mayig corpus is maintained. Contributions stay falsifiable and are judged by evidence, not by volume.

What this is not

  • Not a place to post ungrounded translations.
  • Not a forum, and not a vote on what the script says.
  • It is a test bench: a bar you must clear.

Why not a forum? Communities like the Vesuvius Challenge work because a scroll has ground truth, ink is either there or it isn't. The Indus has no such oracle, so an open discussion forum inevitably fills with confident, unfalsifiable, politically charged claims. A benchmark you must beat is the honest version of collaboration: it invites contribution while keeping evidence, not opinion, as the arbiter.

08 · what it was like

If no one can read it, how did they trade?

The translation problem is ours, not theirs. The Indus people spoke their language fluently for a thousand years; only the written signs fell silent. Trade ran on interpreters (an Akkadian seal names a professional "Meluhhan interpreter"), standardized weights, and the seals themselves, cargo signatures readable by anyone in the system, rather like your signature works abroad today.

Questions we love that we cannot fully answer.

If they had no coins, how did they price things?
Probably by weight and trust. The standardized stone weights let a trader in one city and a trader in another agree on quantities without a shared language or a currency. How value itself was reckoned, we do not know.
Did everyone write, or only a few?
The seals cluster with trade and administration, so writing may have been the tool of a literate minority. Most people may never have written a sign. We cannot be sure.
Why no grand temples or royal tombs?
Nobody has found them. The footprint looks strikingly flat: no palaces, no lavish burials, no monuments to a ruler. Whether that means shared power, hidden hierarchy, or something we have no word for is genuinely open.
What language did they speak at home?
The structure points to the Dravidian family, but the sound is unknown, and across so vast an area there may have been more than one language. The script gives us a frame, not a voice.
Why did the writing stop and never return?
The cities de-urbanized around 1900 BCE, and the script seems to have vanished with the administrative system that needed it. When writing returned to the subcontinent centuries later, it was a completely different system.
Was there one Indus people, or many?
The area is enormous, so almost certainly many, with shared crafts and standards. "The Indus" is our label for a system, not necessarily a single people who would have called themselves one thing.
09 · before & after the indus

The Indus was a chapter, not the beginning.

The Indus Valley Civilisation was not the birth of India. It was one chapter, a spectacular urban one, in a subcontinent already populated, farming, and building for thousands of years before the first Harappan brick, and one that continued, transformed, long after the cities emptied. People did not appear with the Indus and vanish with it.
Tap any ⚠ marker on the timeline to see exactly where scholars disagree.
The old story: India began with the Indus, and when the Indus fell its people vanished or were replaced. The evidence says otherwise. For millennia before Mohenjo-daro, people farmed by the Ganges and herded cattle in the Deccan. During the cities' height, entirely separate cultures thrived across the peninsula. And when the rivers failed, the city-dwellers did not disappear, they moved, mixed, and carried on. The Indus was a brilliant chapter. It was never the first page, and its ending was never the end.

Who were the people? What DNA shows, and what it cannot

  • The deepest layer, the AASI (Ancient Ancestral South Indians), is a deeply diverged lineage indigenous to the subcontinent for tens of thousands of years, distantly related to the Andaman Islanders. South India rests on this deep foundation, not on displaced northerners.
  • The one sequenced Indus genome (Rakhigarhi, ~2600 BCE) carried AASI ancestry plus an Iranian-related lineage that had separated from Iranian populations before farming began, long resident in South Asia, not incoming farmers, and zero steppe ancestry. [Shinde et al. 2019, Cell]
  • Steppe ("Aryan"-associated) ancestry appears after ~2000 BCE, reaching the Swat Valley by ~1200 BCE, as gradual migration and admixture, not invasion and not population replacement. [Narasimhan et al. 2019, Science, 523 ancient genomes]
  • So the honest correction: it is not that Indus people simply migrated south and became Dravidians. Southern populations descend overwhelmingly from the deep AASI layer that predates the Indus; some Iranian-related ancestry blended south gradually over centuries. Parallel, deeply-rooted populations mixing slowly, not one centre seeding everywhere.

The hard limit

Ancient DNA reveals ancestry and movement. It cannot tell us what language anyone spoke, no genome encodes a language. Claims that the Rakhigarhi DNA "proves" the Indus spoke Sanskrit or Dravidian are rejected by the very scientists who produced the data. We report the genetics and stop there. The language question is approached, only partially, through the script's own structure, which is what the rest of this site is about.

  2. Lahuradewa (Ganges): rice by ~6409 BCE (Tewari et al. 2008). ⚠ Whether it was domesticated is disputed, Fuller et al. argue the grains overlap wild rice; fully domesticated rice appears only ~1800 BCE (Mahagara). Stated here as "early rice exploitation."

10 · what would change this

What would change this?

A finding is only science if something could overturn it. Here is what would, and what each would let us finally say.

Suppose

a bilingual seal turns up, an Indus text beside a script we can already read.

Then

we could finally attach sounds, and this whole site would gain the chapter it cannot write today.

Suppose

the full corpus is digitized and a sign we call rare turns out to be common after all.

Then

some signs now written off as undecipherable might quietly become readable.

Suppose

more graffiti from the far south of the subcontinent is analyzed.

Then

we could test whether the script's structure spread beyond the cities, and how far.

Suppose

someone builds an annotated running-text corpus of Gondi or another Dravidian branch.

Then

we could test whether the Indus sits between Dravidian branches, the one question the present data cannot decide.

None of these is promised. All are testable. That is what keeps this a science and not a story.

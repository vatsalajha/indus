# Figure captions

**Figure 1 — Sign frequencies follow a Zipf-like law.** Each dot is one sign,
placed by its rank (1 = most frequent) and how often it occurs; both axes are
logarithmic. Black circles: the 179-seal CISI corpus used in this study; gray
squares: the independent 53-text ICIT preview corpus. The curved line is a
Zipf–Mandelbrot fit (the standard mathematical description of word frequencies
in natural languages) to the CISI data; as in natural languages, the fit is
best in the mid-to-low frequency range. The most frequent sign in every corpus
is the "jar" sign (M77 no. 342 / ICIT no. 740), at about 10% of all sign
occurrences.

**Figure 2 — Signs have fixed jobs in fixed places.** Each marker is one sign
from the CISI corpus (marker size = frequency). Horizontal axis: the sign's
average position within inscriptions, read right-to-left (0 = beginning,
1 = end). Vertical axis: how often the sign stands in the final position.
The marker shape shows the "slot class" assigned by a simple automatic rule.
The terminal signs (down-triangles) — jar, lance, tree, bearer, man — separate
cleanly from the openers (up-triangles, e.g. the diamond and spoked-wheel) and
from the body of the inventory. This is Mahadevan's terminal-sign phenomenon,
recovered by computation alone.

**Figure 3 — The sign order is grammatical, not decorative.** Share of
inscriptions whose sign order obeys a five-slot template (opener → early/
numeral → body → pre-suffix → suffix). Solid bars: the real inscriptions.
Hatched bars: the same inscriptions with sign order randomly shuffled
(average of 1,000 shuffles). Real order beats shuffled order decisively in
both corpora (permutation p < 0.001). The ICIT-preview control bar is high
because only ten signs there are frequent enough to classify — a small-sample
caveat, stated rather than hidden.

**Figure 4 — Signs used in the same contexts.** Darker cells = two signs that
occur with more similar left and right neighbors (cosine similarity of
context profiles), for the 25 most frequent CISI signs. The dark cells in the
jar sign's row (top) against the tree (P086) and lance (P217) show the
functional interchangeability of the terminal signs — found without any
human labeling.

**Figure 5 — No evidence (yet) of regional difference.** Five-slot template
coverage with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals, for the two sites with
enough texts in the preview corpus. Both sites overlap the overall interval;
with n ≤ 27 per site this test is underpowered, and is included as an honest
null result and a placeholder for the full-corpus rerun.

**Figure 6 — What the numerals count.** The signs that most often follow a
stroke numeral (the near-universally accepted number signs), CISI corpus.
Filled bars are fish signs: a quarter of everything counted is a fish —
the distribution Parpola's numeral-+-*mīn* (fish/star) reading predicts.
Numerals also precede other numerals (P145), reflecting compound numbers.

**Figure A — Language placement.** The separable-stacking rate on a common
axis: the fraction of units expressing two or more distinct grammatical
markers. Vedic Sanskrit (gold-annotated) sits at zero — it fuses case, number,
and gender into one ending. Tamil (gold) and both Indus corpora sit well away
from that fusional pole. Bars are 95% bootstrap confidence intervals. Gondi, a
second Dravidian branch, is agglutinative by descriptive grammar but has no
annotated corpus for a comparable rate. This is the figure behind the
"Dravidian, not Indo-Aryan" result.

**Figure B — The rebus null.** Each identifiable sign by how confidently its
pictogram is a real-world object (x-axis) and its verdict (o = the picture is
used literally and matches position; x = no reading). The three high-confidence
fish signs read as literal counted nouns; no sign earns a non-literal phonetic
rebus that survives a frequency-matched null model. The celebrated fish = star
reading is wounded: the mīn homophone is real in the dictionary, but the sign's
position is that of a commodity, not a named entity.

**Figure C — The grammar replicates at scale.** Share of texts conforming to
the five-slot template, observed order versus shuffled control, on the
seal-by-seal primary corpus (175 texts) and the independent ICIT robustness
corpus (2,511 texts). The result holds at fourteen times the scale, in a
different numbering system (permutation p < 0.001 in both).

**Figure D — Integrity scorecard.** Every claim in the study, marked by whether
it survived our own falsification: six survive, two are wounded by our own tests
(fish = star; any surviving phonetic rebus), two are honest nulls, two were
withdrawn on gold data (reduced morphology; distinguishable contact), and the
large secondary corpus is only partially verified. A project that reports its
own wounds and withdrawals, not only its wins.
